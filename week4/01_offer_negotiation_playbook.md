# 💼 Offer Negotiation Playbook

> **Read this BEFORE the offer arrives, not after.**
> Most candidates leave $10K–$30K on the table because they react instead of plan.

---

## 🎯 First principle

The recruiter wants you to accept. The hiring manager wants you to accept. The team wants you to accept. They've already spent 20+ hours on you. Their cost of starting over is enormous. **You have more leverage than you feel.**

Acting calmly and asking for more is *expected behavior* from a strong candidate. Recruiters are trained for it. Refusing to negotiate signals "low confidence" — actively bad for you.

---

## 📞 Phase 1 — When the offer call comes (10 min)

The recruiter will call. They'll say a number. Three rules:

### Rule 1 — Do NOT accept on the call.

The script:

> *"Wow, thank you so much — that means a lot. I really appreciate the offer and I'm excited about the team. I'd love to take 24-48 hours to review the full package and come back with any questions. When's a good time to reconnect on Wednesday?"*

This is **expected** language. Recruiters hear it 10 times a day. Saying it does NOT cost you anything.

### Rule 2 — Get the offer in writing.

> *"Could you send the full offer in writing — base, bonus target, equity, sign-on, and benefits summary — so I can review it carefully?"*

You can't negotiate a number you only heard verbally.

### Rule 3 — Ask one question only — about *their* timeline.

> *"To make sure I respect your process — when do you need an answer?"*

This gives you the negotiation window without asking *for* anything.

## 🎯 Phase 3 — Decide what to ask for

The general formula for a counter-offer:

```
Counter-base = Their offer × 1.10  (max ×1.15)
```

**Example:** They offer $115K base. You ask for $125K-$130K.

**Rules of thumb:**
- Asking for **5-15% more** is universally reasonable
- Asking for **>15%** requires a competing offer or unique credential
- **Sign-on bonus** is the easiest line to move (recruiters can often add $10K-$20K with one approval)
- **Equity** is harder to move at the IC level
- **Title** is sometimes movable and worth asking — Senior vs IC titles compound for the next decade

---

## 📞 Phase 4 — The counter call (10 min)

**Get on a phone call.** Do not negotiate over email. Email negotiation is slower, lower-information, and harder to read tone.

The script (memorize the structure):

> *"Hi [Recruiter Name] — thanks again for sending over the offer. I'm really excited about [Company] and the team — I want to make this work.*
>
> *I've spent some time looking at the package and benchmarking against my conversations with other companies. The base salary is the main piece I'd like to talk through. Based on what I'm seeing for similar roles in the Bay Area for someone with my profile — applied ML, published research, open-source work — I was hoping the base could come up to **$[X]**.*
>
> *Is there room to revisit that number?"*

Then **STOP TALKING.**

The first one to talk after the ask, loses. They will counter, ask for justification, or say they need to check. All of those are good outcomes. Silence is not awkward — it's leverage.

---

## ✋ Phase 5 — Handling pushback

### Pushback A: "We have a band, this is the top of it."
Response: *"I hear that. Could we look at sign-on or equity to bridge the gap?"*

(80% of the time this works. Sign-on is a different budget line.)

### Pushback B: "What's your competing offer?"
**Never lie.** If you don't have one:
> *"I don't have a written offer, but I'm in late stages with [Company X] and [Company Y]. The conversation I'm having with [X] is structured around [number]. I want to be transparent — this is the conversation, not a written offer."*

If you have one:
> *"I have a [verbal/written] offer from [Company] at [number]. I'd genuinely prefer [Lumentum] for [reason]."*

### Pushback C: "We'll have to take it back to the hiring manager."
Response: *"Of course — totally understand. What's the realistic timeline to hear back?"*

(They'll come back. The system is designed for this.)

### Pushback D: "We can't do more on base, but…"
Listen to what they offer. If sign-on goes up by $10K-$15K, that's often the same NPV as base because sign-on is paid upfront. Take it.

---

## 🎁 Things you can ask for besides base

These are often easier wins than base:

- **Sign-on bonus** ($10K-$30K typical)
- **Relocation** ($5K-$15K, especially if you're moving for the role)
- **Earlier review date** (6-month review instead of 12)
- **Earlier equity vesting cliff** (3-month instead of 12-month)
- **Title change** (Sr Data Scientist vs Data Scientist)
- **Remote/hybrid flexibility**
- **Conference travel budget** ($3K-$5K/year)
- **Education stipend** ($2K-$5K/year)
- **Dedicated 20% research time**

---

## ⚠️ What NOT to do

- ❌ Negotiate over text/Slack
- ❌ Get emotional ("I really need…")
- ❌ Threaten or hint at withdrawing
- ❌ Make up competing offers
- ❌ Ask for everything at once — pick 1-2 priorities
- ❌ Counter-offer twice. **One counter only.** Their response is the final number.

---

## 💌 Phase 6 — Closing

Once you've agreed:

> *"Great. To confirm: base $[X], sign-on $[Y], equity $[Z], title [T], start date [D]. I'll be ready to formally accept once I see the updated written offer. Thanks for working with me on this — really excited to join the team."*

Make them email the updated offer. Sign it. Move on.

**Then send a thank-you note** to the hiring manager (separate from the recruiter):

> *"Hi [HM] — just signed the updated offer. Thank you for everything throughout this process. Really looking forward to starting on [date]. Let me know if there's any prep you'd recommend before day one."*

---

## 🪙 If the offer is from a smaller company / startup

The rules shift slightly:

- **Base salary** has less room (smaller budgets)
- **Equity** has more room — and matters more
- **Title** is much easier to move
- **Total comp comparison** must include equity at fully-diluted ownership %, not nominal share count
- **Beware:** "We pay below market but you'll learn a lot" is a red flag at this stage of your career. The first 5 years of comp compound for the next 30. Don't underprice yourself for a learning-curve story.

---

## 💎 The mindset

Negotiation is not a confrontation. It's a **conversation about fit**. You are signaling: *"I take this seriously, I know my worth, I plan to bring my full self to this role."* Companies want to hire people who advocate for themselves — those people advocate for the company later.

**Negotiating well makes you more attractive, not less.**

You earned this. Ask for the number. Trust the process.
