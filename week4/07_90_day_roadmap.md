## 🅱️ Path B — Final-stage interview, no offer yet

Most likely outcome. Here's how June plays out.

### June Week 1

- [ ] Continue active interview processes — calmly, not anxiously
- [ ] **Reduce posting frequency** to 1 post / 10 days. The campaign goal is met; now you're maintaining surface area, not growing it
- [ ] **Increase 1:1 outreach** — DMs > posts at this stage
- [ ] **Stay sharp** — review System Design / SQL / ML interview prep daily for 30 min

### June Week 2

- [ ] If a final-round outcome is delayed, **send a polite check-in** at the 7-day mark, not before
- [ ] If timeline is unclear, **start a parallel late-stage process** elsewhere — leverage matters
- [ ] Keep the repo & paper warm with one bug-fix or doc improvement / week — you'll mention this in interviews

### June Week 3-4

- [ ] By June 30, you should have a **decision** from the late-stage process — yes, no, or named delay
- [ ] If no offer by June 30, switch to Path C below

### Mental model

This phase is **the long bet**. Most senior data science / applied ML hires take 6-12 weeks from first contact to offer. You're inside that window. Calm + sustained = wins. Anxiety + churn = loses.

Send fewer, better-targeted DMs. Ship one substantive technical post per 10 days. Don't repeat the launch — the launch was the launch.

## 🅲️ Path C — No offer in sight yet

Don't panic. Most data science / applied ML new-grad searches take **3-6 months**, not 4 weeks. You're at week 4. The work has been done. Now you're playing for momentum.

### Strategic pivots to choose ONE of:

#### Pivot 1 — Expand role aperture
You've been targeting Bay Area, applied ML, mid-tier+ companies. Open up:
- ✅ Remote roles
- ✅ Full-stack data science (not just ML)
- ✅ Earlier-stage companies (Series A/B)
- ✅ Data engineer / analytics engineer roles (which many MSBAs land first and pivot from)
- ✅ Adjacent industries — fintech, biotech, climate tech

#### Pivot 2 — Geographic expansion
- ✅ Remote everywhere
- ✅ NYC / Boston / Seattle (similar comp band)
- ✅ Hybrid in 2nd-tier markets — Austin, Denver, Raleigh
- ✅ International (Toronto / Vancouver / London — visa-dependent)

#### Pivot 3 — Niche down hard
The opposite move. Stop applying broadly. Pick:
- One vertical (ex: people analytics, given AttriSense)
- 5-10 named companies
- Dedicate 4 weeks to deep, named outreach to each

This often works after the broad-net month, because you now have artifacts to make a vertical-specific case.

#### Pivot 4 — Take a contract / consulting bridge
- ✅ MLOps / data engineering 3-month contracts (Toptal, Catalant)
- ✅ Open-source-foundation paid fellowships (Apache, NumFOCUS)
- ✅ University research-engineer positions at CSUEB or another local school
- These provide income + credibility while continuing the full-time search

#### Pivot 5 — Accept that the search continues, and rest
Sometimes the right answer is: take 2 weeks fully off. Travel. Read non-tech books. Then return to the search refreshed in mid-June. The candidates who close in months 4-6 of the search are usually the ones who paced themselves, not the ones who burned out by month 3.

## 💎 The hardest mindset to hold (and the most important)

**This is a process problem, not a quality problem.**

Your project is real. Your paper is published. Your skills are genuine. The work is good.

What's left is variance — the random walk of which company has a budget approved this week, which hiring manager is on vacation, which recruiter happens to be looking. None of that is about you.

The candidates who close offers in months 4-6 are the same candidates who applied in months 1-3 and didn't quit. The work doesn't get redone. The pipeline just takes time.

**Three rules for the long search:**

1. **Don't lose your weekends.** A burned-out search is slower than a paced search.
2. **Track input, not output.** Number of DMs sent, conversations had, applications submitted. Outcomes are downstream and lagging.
3. **Have one non-job-search thing each week.** Friends, family, gym, art, anything. Identity matters.
