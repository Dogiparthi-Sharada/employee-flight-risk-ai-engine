================================================================================
  PART 1 — THE FOUNDATIONAL PROMPTS
================================================================================

These are the prompts that produced the major artifacts. Copy, adapt, reuse.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PROMPT 1 — The opening request (produced LINKEDIN_POST.md)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  USER: "I want to clone https://github.com/Dogiparthi-Sharada/
  employee-flight-risk-ai-engine and write a premium LinkedIn post
  for Sharada. She's an MSBA candidate at Cal State East Bay
  interviewing at Lumentum. Make it stand out."

WHY THIS WORKED:
  • Specific repo (gives the AI ground truth)
  • Specific person, specific credential (MSBA, CSUEB)
  • Specific company target (Lumentum)
  • One word sets the quality bar ("premium")

LESSON FOR YOU:
  Always include: who, what credential, what target, what tone.
  Vague prompts produce vague output.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PROMPT 2 — Reframing the AI-built concern (produced AI_CHEATSHEET.md)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  USER: "Sharada is worried she will be asked in the interview
  whether she really built this — most of the project was AI-
  built. How do we handle this honestly?"

WHY THIS WORKED:
  • Honest about the underlying concern
  • Asks for an honest solution, not a cover story
  • Implicit constraint: must work in a real interview

OUTPUT PATTERN:
  AI gave the "Yes — Copilot + Claude as collaborators" framing.
  Honesty + ownership + judgment-as-moat. Better than any spin.

LESSON FOR YOU:
  The best AI output happens when YOU set the integrity bar.
  If you ask the AI to "make me sound impressive" you get fluff.
  If you ask "give me an honest answer to a hard question" you get
  language you can actually defend.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PROMPT 3 — The expansion (produced the IEEE paper + 4-week campaign)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  USER: "Make me an IEEE paper. Then build week 1, week 2, week 3,
  and week 4 collateral as well."

WHY THIS WORKED:
  • Sequential (paper first, campaign next)
  • Asks for a SYSTEM, not a piece
  • Implicit: AI must hold context across the whole arc

LESSON FOR YOU:
  When you want big output, ask for the whole system.
  AI is excellent at scaffolding. It is bad at filling in gaps
  when you give it isolated pieces and ask it to imagine the rest.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PROMPT 4 — The constraint: real environment (produced build_docx.py)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  USER: "Use this venv: /global/gtsna_northeast6/vanama/python_venvs/py311.
  Generate me a real .docx — not LaTeX I have to compile."

WHY THIS WORKED:
  • Specific environment path
  • Specific output format
  • Removes a common AI failure mode (assuming the user has tools
    they don't actually have)

LESSON FOR YOU:
  Always tell the AI:
    - What tools you have
    - What tools you DON'T have
    - What format you actually need
  AI defaults assume Mac + Linux + LaTeX + a tech-bro setup. Override.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PROMPT 5 — Today's request (produced the TODAY/ folder)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  USER: "She is having interview today and wants to post and
  walk in with a demo. How do you say we need to post later?
  Think through what hurdles she will face. Create a Windows-
  friendly zip. Tell me ways to share without triggering
  company issues."

WHY THIS WORKED:
  • Multiple distinct asks bundled, but each is concrete
  • Gives the AI permission to disagree with the user's plan
    ("How do you say we need to post later?")
  • Asks for opinion AND execution

LESSON FOR YOU:
  Give AI permission to push back. The best line in this prompt
  was "how do you say we need to post later?" — it invited
  contrary advice. Most users only ask AI to AGREE with them.
  You'll get smarter answers if you ask it to challenge you.


================================================================================
  PART 2 — REUSABLE PROMPT TEMPLATES
================================================================================

For any future AI work — Copilot Chat, Claude, ChatGPT — fill these in.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TEMPLATE A — Generate honest interview answers
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  "I have a job interview at [COMPANY] for a [ROLE] role tomorrow.
   The interviewer is likely to ask: '[HARD QUESTION]'.

   My honest position is: [ONE SENTENCE TRUTH].

   Give me 3 ways to answer this honestly that ALSO position me well
   as a candidate. Avoid spin or evasion. Each answer should be 2-4
   sentences and include a follow-up I can offer voluntarily."

WHY: produces real, defensible answers — not corporate-speak.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TEMPLATE B — Build a multi-part document
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  "I need to build [DOCUMENT TYPE] for [PURPOSE].
   Audience: [SPECIFIC PERSON OR ROLE].
   Length: [PAGES OR WORDS].
   Tone: [3 ADJECTIVES — e.g. 'calm, technical, generous'].
   Constraints: [WHAT MUST BE INCLUDED / EXCLUDED].
   Reference my prior work: [LINKS OR FILE PATHS].

   First, give me a section-by-section outline.
   I'll approve, then we'll write each section."

WHY: forces structure before content. Massively higher quality.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TEMPLATE C — Pressure-test a decision
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  "I'm planning to [DECISION].
   The reason is [REASONING].
   The expected outcome is [OUTCOME].

   Please:
   1. List the strongest case AGAINST this decision
   2. Identify the assumption most likely to be wrong
   3. Suggest a smaller, reversible test
   4. Tell me if you think I should proceed"

WHY: AI defaults to agreeing. This forces it to argue. Use before
any irreversible move.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TEMPLATE D — Convert technical work to non-technical narrative
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  "I built [TECHNICAL THING]. The technical version is:
   [TWO PARAGRAPHS OF JARGON].

   Translate this for [AUDIENCE — e.g. 'a hiring manager who has
   an MBA but no ML background']. Keep it specific. Use one
   concrete example. Stay under [N] words."

WHY: The translation skill is the single rarest interview skill.
This template produces it consistently.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TEMPLATE E — Write a thank-you / follow-up email
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  "I just had a [INTERVIEW/CALL/MEETING] with [NAME] at [COMPANY].
   We discussed: [3 BULLETS — what was actually said].
   They mentioned: [ONE SPECIFIC THING THEY SAID].

   Draft a thank-you email that:
   - References that specific thing
   - Adds one piece of value (link / answer / resource)
   - Has a low-pressure ask
   - Stays under 150 words
   - Sounds like ME, not corporate-speak"

WHY: specificity > template. The "one specific thing" line is the
single highest-conversion element of any follow-up email.


================================================================================
  PART 3 — KEY DECISIONS THIS CONVERSATION MADE
================================================================================

Annotated, so you can re-derive them yourself next time.


DECISION 1: Project rename "employee-flight-risk-ai-engine" → "attrisense"

  Why: shorter, brandable, googleable, and memorable.
  Trade-off: breaks any old GitHub link. Acceptable cost.
  Reusable: when naming any side-project, optimize for "can someone
            type this from memory after one hearing?"


DECISION 2: Paper venue: IEEE Big Data Industry Track (1st choice)

  Why: ~30% accept rate, 8-week review, applied focus, AI-disclosure
       permitted, 6-10 page limit matches our draft.
  Backup: arXiv first (always), IEEE Access journal, INFORMS J. of
          Applied Analytics.
  Reusable: pick the venue with highest accept × prestige × speed,
            not the most prestigious.


DECISION 3: AI-disclosure: full transparency, every artifact

  Why: norm-shifting fast. Hiding it = career risk. Disclosing it
       well = differentiation. The cost of lying is permanent.
  Reusable: when norms are shifting, lead the new norm — don't
            hide behind the old one.


DECISION 4: 4-week launch, Tuesday May 12 as launch day

  Why: Tuesday morning is peak LinkedIn traffic. Pre-launch (this
       week) is for polish, not posting. Post-launch (weeks 3-4)
       compounds.
  Reusable: spectacular single-day launches < sustained 4-week
            sequences.


DECISION 5: TODAY (May 1) — interview, NO public posting

  Why: posting before an interview is asymmetric risk — the upside
       is small, the downside is permanent association of the post
       with the interview outcome.
  Reusable: never broadcast a major artifact within 48 hours of
            a high-stakes 1:1 conversation about that artifact.


DECISION 6: Patent path = CSUEB Tech Transfer first; self-file ($75) second

  Why: institutional backing > self-file. But $75 self-file is real
       when institution declines. Don't drop the angle without trying.
  Reusable: in any IP question, ask the institution that owes you
            something (your school) before the institution that
            doesn't (the USPTO directly).


================================================================================
  PART 4 — THINGS THE AI GOT RIGHT THAT YOU SHOULD STEAL
================================================================================

Patterns from the output that you should recognize and reuse.


PATTERN 1: Specificity over polish

  Bad:  "high-performing predictive model"
  Good: "PR-AUC 0.74, ROC-AUC 0.91, Brier 0.061 on a 10%-prevalence
         imbalanced dataset"

  Apply this to your resume, interview answers, LinkedIn posts.


PATTERN 2: Naming the limitation up front

  "It's correlational, not causal. The data is synthetic."

  This single move turns "the candidate is hiding things" into
  "the candidate is rigorous." Always lead with the limitation.


PATTERN 3: The vulnerable mid-section

  In every long post / playbook, there's a section labeled
  "what I got wrong." Vulnerability posts outperform success posts
  on LinkedIn because they're rare, specific, and humanizing.

  Apply: every artifact should have one honest-failure section.


PATTERN 4: Clear next step at the end of every document

  Every playbook ends with: "Sunday, do X." or "First DM tomorrow
  is Y." Vague advice doesn't compile to action. The AI was forced
  to specify because the prompt asked for it.

  Apply: every email, every conversation, every post — close with
  a concrete next step. The other party should never have to invent
  one.


PATTERN 5: Permission to skip

  The Saturday May 31 carousel is explicitly OPTIONAL. The 12 v2
  features are "pick one." The 4-week roadmap accepts that Path C
  (no offer yet) is a real possibility.

  Apply: realistic plans beat ambitious-and-impossible plans.
  Build with skip-conditions baked in.


================================================================================
  PART 5 — WHAT TO DO IF YOU RUN THIS PLAYBOOK AGAIN
================================================================================

Future you, doing this for another project or another interview.


STEP 1: Don't start with the LinkedIn post. Start with the project.

   Order matters. If the project is real, the post writes itself.
   If the project is thin, no post can save it.


STEP 2: Use AI for scaffolding, not for substance.

   AI is excellent at: outlines, templates, first drafts, reviews,
                     translations, structure.
   AI is bad at:    novel insight, taste, your specific voice,
                    your specific situation, real-world judgment.

   Always: AI drafts. You revise. AI cleans. You ship.


STEP 3: Save your prompts.

   Best prompts compound. The Template B / D / E above came from
   3 weeks of iteration — and they'll work for the next 30
   projects. Keep a personal prompts.md file.


STEP 4: Review every AI output for these failure modes:

   ❌ Generic praise of yourself (AI loves this; it's empty)
   ❌ Made-up statistics or citations (always verify)
   ❌ Em-dashes everywhere (AI tic; remove or replace)
   ❌ Lists of 7+ items where 3 would do (AI inflates)
   ❌ Hedging language: "may", "might", "could potentially"
      (replace with definite statements when you're certain)


STEP 5: When in doubt — read it aloud.

   The AI cannot hear your voice. You can. Anything that sounds
   awkward when spoken should be rewritten before it ships.
